[Frankopan](/authors/peter-frankopan)'s audacious thesis: we have been telling history wrong. By centering civilization's story on Western Europe, we've missed the real axis of human development — the vast trade and cultural networks stretching from the Mediterranean through Persia, Central Asia, and China. The Silk Roads were a living system of exchange that shaped empires, spread religions, transmitted plague, and moved ideas as fluidly as goods. The sweep is genuinely staggering — from ancient Persia to the War on Terror — and Frankopan carries it off with confident, engaging prose. History looks different from the middle of the world.

Readers captivated by Frankopan's reorientation of history should also explore [Roger Crowley](/authors/crowley)'s [Conquerors](/reviews/conquerors) for the Portuguese maritime story, and [Genghis Khan and the Making of the Modern World](/reviews/genghis-khan-and-the-making-of-the-modern-world) by [Jack Weatherford](/authors/jack-weatherford) for the Mongol chapter.
