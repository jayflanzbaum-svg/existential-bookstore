House of Leaves is a puzzle box designed to give you vertigo. At its center is an impossible house: a house in Virginia whose interior dimensions exceed its exterior dimensions by a fraction of an inch, then by a foot, then by a hallway that shouldn't exist, then by a staircase that descends for miles. Around this impossible geometry Danielewski builds a nested narrative structure: a documentary about the house, annotations on the documentary by a dead old man, and the found journals of the young man who discovered those annotations.

The footnotes are not optional. They are where some of the most important — and most unsettling — material lives. The typography itself becomes a tool of horror: words scattered across nearly empty pages, text columns that spiral inward, pages so dense with citations that the fiction and the apparatus become indistinguishable.

I am reluctant to give away more. The experience of reading House of Leaves is irreducible to summary. What I will say is that its central horror — the horror of a space that does not behave as space should, that does not respect the rules by which you have organized your sense of the possible — is the most original in contemporary fiction. You may need to put it down in the middle. That is appropriate.
