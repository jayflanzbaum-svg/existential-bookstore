Meursault is not a monster. This is the unsettling thing. He is a man who has decided to live only in the immediate present, to feel what he feels and describe what he sees without the overlay of social meaning that the rest of us apply automatically. He does not perform grief at his mother's funeral because he does not feel it in the way that is expected. He pulls the trigger on the beach because the sun is in his eyes and his hand already holds the gun.

Camus called The Stranger an exercise in the absurd — by which he meant the collision between humanity's need for meaning and the universe's indifference to that need. Meursault embodies the collision. He has opted out of the meaning-making. He has stopped pretending.

The novel is 123 pages and reads in an afternoon. The first sentence — Mother died today. Or maybe yesterday, I don't know — is among the most efficient scene-setting openings in the canon. By the end, when Meursault makes his peace with the universe's silence, you may find yourself more moved than you expected by a man who never asked you to be moved at all.
