There is a version of yourself that only exists in motion. Kerouac understood this before anyone had given it a name. On the Road is not a travel book. It is a book about the hunger that makes certain people incapable of staying still — the hunger for experience, for connection, for some truth that seems always just around the next bend in the highway.

Sal Paradise watches Dean Moriarty consume the world whole — the jazz clubs and diners and gas stations and wide-open nights of postwar America — and asks himself whether he can afford not to follow. The novel reads like it was written on a single long exhale, which is more or less how it was. Kerouac typed it on a single 120-foot scroll of paper in three weeks, running the raw material of his cross-country trips through the machine of his memory.

What he produced was something that felt to its first readers like a message from another country: a country where you were allowed to want more, to leave, to fail gloriously, to start again. I read it at nineteen and felt the pull like gravity. I have never fully stopped feeling it.
