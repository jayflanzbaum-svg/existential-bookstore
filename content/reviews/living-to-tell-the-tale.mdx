Before [García Márquez](/authors/marquez) wrote [One Hundred Years of Solitude](/reviews/one-hundred-years-of-solitude)—what I consider the greatest story ever told—he lived a life that reads like one of his novels. Living to Tell the Tale is his memoir, and it is the rare book that helps you understand not just a writer, but an entire world. He grew up in the sweltering heat of coastal Colombia, surrounded by storytellers, ghosts, and the kind of poverty that somehow produces beauty. Reading this, you understand where the yellow flowers came from. You understand why the rain never stops in his stories, why the dead walk alongside the living, why love and madness are indistinguishable. This is a book about how a writer becomes a writer, but more than that, it is a book about how a place shapes a soul. If you have ever fallen under the spell of magical realism, this is where you go to understand its roots.

For the full spectrum of García Márquez's genius, pair this memoir with [Strange Pilgrims](/reviews/strange-pilgrims), his haunting collection of displaced Latin Americans in Europe.
