If On the Road is Kerouac at full velocity — hungry, reckless, burning through America — The Dharma Bums is Kerouac beginning to slow down and look up. Published a year after On the Road in 1958, it follows Ray Smith and Japhy Ryder (based on the poet Gary Snyder) through California, Mexico, and finally into the mountains of Washington State, where Ray spends a summer as a fire lookout on a peak called Desolation.

The Buddhism in the novel is genuine but worn lightly. Japhy is the serious student; Ray is the enthusiast who absorbs what he can and carries it loosely. The conversations about the dharma — about detachment, impermanence, the emptiness of form — are interspersed with wine and parties and mountain climbing and the ordinary pleasures of being young and broke and free.

What stays with me is the final section on Desolation Peak: seventy pages of a man alone with mountains and sky and the slowly unwinding noise of his own mind. Kerouac was no saint and made no claims to be one. But the mountain quieted him, briefly. You can feel it on the page.
