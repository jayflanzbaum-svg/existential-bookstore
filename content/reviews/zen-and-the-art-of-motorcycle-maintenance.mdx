This book changed how I think about making things. Robert [Pirsig](/authors/robert-m-pirsig)'s account of a cross-country motorcycle trip with his son is really a philosophical investigation into the nature of Quality—what makes something good, what separates craftsmanship from mere competence, and why the Western habit of separating romantic and rational ways of knowing the world has left us impoverished. I came to this book as someone who builds things—who tinkers with electronics and woodwork and radio antennas—and found in it an articulation of something I had felt but never been able to name. There is a kind of meditation that happens when you are working carefully on something with your hands, when you are fully present in the problem. One of the most important books I have ever read.

Pirsig returned to these themes seventeen years later in [Lila: An Inquiry into Morals](/reviews/lila-an-inquiry-into-morals), which extends his Metaphysics of Quality into a full philosophical system—a more mature, if less personal, companion to this journey.
