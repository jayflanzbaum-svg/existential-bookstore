Goldsworthy gives us a dual biography arguing that Alexander the Great cannot be understood without his father. Philip II transformed Macedonia from a minor northern kingdom into the dominant power of the Greek world, forging the army, treasury, and diplomatic framework that made Alexander's conquests possible. Goldsworthy writes with clarity and authority, never losing the narrative thread amid complex ancient sources. He is admirably skeptical of romantic legend while attuned to the genuine drama of two extraordinary lives. Alexander emerges as brilliant but increasingly unhinged, the tragic inheritor of a machine built for conquest he couldn't stop.

For the broader story of how Alexander's conquests opened the trade routes between East and West, [The Silk Roads](/reviews/the-silk-roads-a-new-history-of-the-world) by [Peter Frankopan](/authors/peter-frankopan) provides the grand narrative that begins where Philip and Alexander's story ends.
