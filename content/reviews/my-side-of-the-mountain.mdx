I first read this book lying on the floor of our living room, probably nine years old, with no idea that a story about a boy living alone in the Catskill Mountains was quietly teaching me something I would spend decades trying to articulate: that solitude is a skill, and like any skill, it rewards practice.

Sam Gribley runs away from his crowded New York City apartment to his great-grandfather's abandoned farm in the mountains. He learns to make fire, train a peregrine falcon he names Frightful, and hollow out a hemlock tree large enough to sleep in. The book is a survival manual disguised as a novel, and it works as both.

What stayed with me wasn't the survival craft, though I memorized it. It was the emotional texture of chosen aloneness — the way George renders Sam's contentment without sentimentality, his loneliness without self-pity. Sam is not running from something. He is running toward a version of himself that the city would never let him find.
