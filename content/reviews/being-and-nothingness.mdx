Sartre published Being and Nothingness in 1943, in occupied Paris, while working as a schoolteacher. The circumstances feel important. The book is an argument against every form of excuse-making, every attempt to claim that one's choices were determined by circumstance, history, or nature. It is a book that insists on your freedom at the moment when freedom was most costly.

The central argument is disarmingly simple: consciousness is nothingness. Unlike objects, which simply are what they are, human consciousness is always a step behind itself, always defining itself through negation, always becoming rather than being. This is what Sartre calls bad faith: pretending to be a fixed thing, a waiter who is only a waiter, a coward who simply is a coward, when in fact one is always choosing.

I will not pretend the book is easy. It is 700 pages of dense phenomenological argument, and Sartre's prose does not reward skimming. But neither will I pretend that its difficulty is without purpose. The difficulty is the point: freedom costs something, and the cost is the effort of thinking it through.
