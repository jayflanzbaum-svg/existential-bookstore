Eugene Carroll's Hunter is a rare find — a novel that inhabits its world with the quiet authority of deep insider knowledge. Carroll, a retired Navy rear admiral, brings unsparing authenticity to his portrait of military culture, bureaucratic inertia, and the moral compromises that accumulate quietly over a career in service. The prose is lean and unsentimental, trusting the reader to draw conclusions. The book operates as both a character study and a critique of institutional power, asking hard questions about what gets rewarded in hierarchical organizations and what gets buried. Not widely known, but worth seeking out.
