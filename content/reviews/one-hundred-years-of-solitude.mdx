The first sentence of this novel is one of the most famous in the Western canon. Many years later, as he faced the firing squad, Colonel Aureliano Buendía was to remember that distant afternoon when his father took him to discover ice. It promises everything — time compressed, wonder alongside violence, the mundane made miraculous — and keeps every promise across nearly four hundred pages.

Macondo is a town that García Márquez invented and then loved into existence. The Buendía family populates it across six generations, each member a variation on themes of solitude, ambition, love, and repetition. The magic of the book is not in its ghosts and levitations — those are just the weather. The magic is in how García Márquez treats extraordinary events with the same calm authority as ordinary ones. A man rises to heaven while hanging laundry. The narrator mentions it the way you might mention rain.

I came to this book at twenty-two and found that it reorganized my understanding of what a novel could do. Not all literature aims to represent reality. Some aims to be more real than reality — to capture the texture of memory and myth and family story, which is always a little more vivid and a little less reliable than the facts.
