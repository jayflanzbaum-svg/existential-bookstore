Vonnegut survived the firebombing of Dresden in a slaughterhouse cellar, emerged to find a city turned to ash, and then spent twenty-three years trying to write a book about it. The book he finally wrote refuses to be a war novel in any conventional sense. Billy Pilgrim has come unstuck in time. He moves between his wartime captivity, his postwar life as an optometrist in upstate New York, and his abduction by aliens from the planet Tralfamadore, who perceive all moments simultaneously and have long since accepted that the universe will end because of a Tralfamadorian experiment gone wrong.

So it goes, says Vonnegut, each time someone dies. The phrase appears over a hundred times. It is simultaneously the most nihilistic and the most tender refrain in American literature — an acknowledgment that death is everywhere and ordinary, and that the only honest response is a kind of rueful, wide-eyed acceptance.

The science fiction is a coping mechanism. Tralfamadore is the place Billy goes because he cannot stand being where he is. The time travel is PTSD described from the inside. The comedy — and there is real comedy — is the only tool Vonnegut has that is still working after Dresden.
